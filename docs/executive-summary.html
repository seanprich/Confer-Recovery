The Problem

Why ordinary video calls put your members at risk

When your chapter holds a meeting on Zoom, Microsoft Teams, or Google Meet, you are renting a conference room from a corporation. The landlord owns the building. Their employees can walk in. Their policies — not yours — govern your members' data.

For most businesses, this is an acceptable trade-off. For chapter meetings where members share personal struggles, health information, and deeply private circumstances, it is not.

What "Terms of Service" usually means in practice Major video platforms reserve the right to access recordings for moderation, legal compliance, fraud detection, product improvement, and law enforcement requests — often without notifying meeting participants. Your members may not realize what they agreed to when they clicked "Accept."

The three risks of rented video platforms

Corporate access. Zoom, Teams, and similar services process your video and audio on their servers. Their staff, under the right circumstances, can access that data. Your chapter has no technical means to prevent this — only the platform's promises.

Data retention you don't control. These platforms store metadata — who attended, when, how long — for periods determined by their business needs, not yours. That data may be shared with business partners, advertisers, or government agencies.

Data breach exposure. A security failure at a large platform can expose data from millions of users simultaneously. Your chapter's information is pooled with everyone else's.

The fundamental question Who do you trust more with your members' most sensitive disclosures — a corporation optimizing for profit and legal compliance, or your own chapter?
The Solution

What is Confer?

Confer is video meeting software built specifically for chapter-based organizations that require strong, verifiable privacy. It is not a service you subscribe to. It is software your chapter owns and runs on its own computer.

What "self-hosted" means in plain English Think of the difference between holding your chapter meeting in a room you rent versus a room you own. In a rented room, the building manager has a key. In your own space, you control who has access — and you can verify it. Confer runs in your chapter's own space.

How it works at a high level

Your chapter installs Confer on a small, affordable computer — typically a device the size of a paperback book — located wherever your chapter stores its equipment. That computer becomes your chapter's private meeting server.

When members join a meeting, their audio and video travel between their devices and your chapter's server — not through any corporation's network. When the meeting ends, nothing leaves. No recording is sent to a cloud service. No usage data is reported to us. The meeting simply ends.

Your chapter owns the server. Your chapter controls access. Your chapter can audit what the system records — because your chapter runs the system.

What Confer is not

It is not a subscription service with a company holding your data. It is not a free tool that generates revenue by analyzing your meetings. It is not a platform that can be acquired by another company and have its privacy policies changed. Once your chapter installs it, the software belongs to your chapter — no ongoing dependency on us required.

Privacy by design, not by policy Most platforms protect privacy through promises and policies. Confer protects privacy through architecture — it is technically incapable of sending your meeting data to a third party because the code was built that way intentionally, not as an afterthought.
Open source means you can verify every claim in this document Confer is free, open-source software licensed under the GNU General Public License. The complete source code is publicly available. You do not have to take anyone's word for how it handles privacy — your chapter's technical staff, an independent developer, or a security researcher can read the code and confirm it directly. No black box. No hidden behavior.
Data & Records

What the system records — and what it never touches

Every record the system keeps is stored exclusively on your chapter's server. There is no external copy, no cloud backup managed by us, and no analytics pipeline sending data anywhere.

What IS recorded

  • Who attended each meeting (member name, date, join and leave time)
  • When meetings were scheduled, started, and ended
  • When a member logged in (date and time only)
  • That a member acknowledged the privacy policy (not what they read)
  • System health information (see page 7)

What is NEVER recorded

  • Audio from any meeting
  • Video from any meeting
  • Chat messages or shared content
  • What was discussed or said by anyone
  • Anything about meeting content

Why attendance records exist

The attendance log — who joined and left each meeting — serves as an accountability record. It cannot be edited or deleted after the fact. This is intentional: it protects members by creating a tamper-proof record of participation, while deliberately excluding the content of what was shared.

Think of it like a sign-in sheet at the door. It records who was present, not what they said once inside.

Your password is one of the most sensitive pieces of data we handle Your password is never stored in a form that can be read — by us, your chapter's administrator, or anyone who might gain unauthorized access to the database. It is transformed through a one-way cryptographic process. The only way to confirm a password is correct is to enter the correct one. If you forget your password, it cannot be retrieved — only reset by an administrator.

How long records are kept

Record retention is entirely your chapter's decision. Your chapter's system administrator manages the database directly. We do not impose retention policies or delete records on your behalf. Your chapter can establish its own retention practices appropriate to your organizational requirements and any applicable legal obligations.

Privacy Protections

Five guarantees we built into the system

These are not policies that can be changed by a future business decision. They are technical properties of how the software was built.

1

Your data never leaves your chapter's server

All meeting data — attendance records, member accounts, room history — is stored only on the computer your chapter operates. We do not maintain any servers that your data passes through after installation. There is no "phone home" mechanism, no telemetry sent to us, no remote backup we manage.

2

Your password cannot be recovered by anyone

Passwords are processed through BCrypt, a deliberately slow and irreversible transformation. Even with full access to your chapter's database, no one — including your chapter's own administrator — can retrieve a member's original password. If lost, it must be reset, never recovered.

3

Consent is required before any meeting access

Before a member can join any meeting for the first time, they must formally acknowledge the privacy policy through the application. This acknowledgment is recorded with a timestamp. Until it is on file, the system will not issue a meeting access pass — regardless of who they are or what role they hold.

4

All access automatically expires

When you log in, you receive a digital pass valid for 60 minutes. When you join a specific meeting, you receive a room-specific pass valid for up to 2 hours. After expiration, you must log in again. There is no "stay logged in forever" option — access is short-lived by design.

5

Connections are encrypted in transit

All communication between member devices and the chapter's server is encrypted — scrambled so that anyone who intercepts the data stream (on a shared network, for example) cannot read it. This applies to login, meeting traffic, and all API communications.

+

Meeting configuration secrets are encrypted at rest

The technical key used to secure the media server connection is stored encrypted in the database using ASP.NET Data Protection — a system-level encryption mechanism. Even with direct database access, this key cannot be read in plaintext.

Access Control

Who can do what — understanding roles

Every member of Confer is assigned exactly one role. That role determines what they can see, do, and access. The system enforces these limits technically — it is not merely a policy that someone could choose to ignore.

Role Typical holder Join meetings Share camera / mic Host meetings Add members Manage platform
Listener Regular members Yes No No No No
Presenter Speakers, facilitators Yes Yes No No No
Host Meeting runners Yes Yes Yes No No
Chapter Admin Chapter leadership Yes Yes Yes Yes No
Org Admin Technical administrator Yes Yes Yes Yes Yes

Two important constraints worth noting

Only an Org Admin can change another person's role. A Chapter Admin cannot promote themselves or anyone else to Org Admin. A Host cannot grant themselves administrative access. Role elevation requires someone who already has the highest level of trust — and that assignment is an explicit, logged action.

The first Org Admin is set up directly in the database. There is no public "sign up as admin" page. The initial Org Admin account is created by whoever installs the system, directly through the database — before the web interface is ever opened. This prevents anyone from claiming administrator access through the web application.

Chapter isolation Each member belongs to exactly one chapter. A member's role and access are scoped to their chapter. A Host in Chapter A cannot see, access, or affect Chapter B's meetings, members, or data.
System Monitoring

How the system monitors its own health

Like any piece of important equipment, your chapter's server needs to be checked regularly to ensure it is running correctly. Confer includes a built-in monitoring dashboard — think of it as the instrument panel on a car, showing you whether the engine is healthy without telling anyone outside the car what you are doing.

What the monitoring dashboard shows

A web page accessible only within your chapter's network — not from the internet — displays live information about the server:

Information tracked by the health monitor

  • Is the server running and responding to requests?
  • How many meetings are currently active?
  • How quickly is the server responding (in milliseconds)?
  • Is the database connected and accessible?
  • How much computer memory is the server using?
  • How many login attempts have occurred (success vs. failure counts only — no names)?
  • How many meeting access passes have been issued?
This monitoring is entirely local Every number on the dashboard is generated by your chapter's server and displayed only within your chapter's network. No monitoring data — not a single metric — is sent to us, to any analytics provider, or to any internet service. The dashboard cannot be accessed from outside your network without your chapter explicitly opening that access.

What the monitoring does not include

The health monitor deliberately excludes anything that could identify a specific member or reveal what happened in a meeting. There are no member names in the monitoring data, no meeting content indicators, no individual login records, and no information that could be used to track a specific person's behavior. The monitoring data describes the health of the machine — not the activities of the people using it.

Who can see the dashboard

Access to the monitoring dashboard requires being connected to your chapter's local network (or VPN). It is not protected by a password by default, so your chapter should restrict network access to it appropriately — accessible to the technical administrator, not to general members.

Common Questions

Questions from privacy-focused members

Can the company that built this software see our meetings?

No. Once installed on your chapter's server, we have no access to your data, your meetings, or your members. We do not operate any servers that your meeting traffic passes through. We cannot receive a law enforcement request for your meeting data because we do not possess it — your chapter does.

Is this more private than Zoom or Microsoft Teams?

For chapter meetings involving sensitive personal disclosures, yes — significantly. Zoom and Teams process your audio and video on servers owned by their respective corporations. Those corporations are subject to law enforcement requests, data breach risks, policy changes, and business decisions that may affect your data. With Confer, your chapter is the only entity with access to your data.

Can meetings be recorded?

Confer does not include a recording feature. The system does not capture or store audio or video from meetings. As with any video platform, a participant could use a third-party application on their own device to record their screen — this is beyond the control of any meeting software and a matter of chapter policy rather than technical capability.

What if someone gains unauthorized access to our server?

Physical and network security of the server is the chapter's responsibility. If the database were accessed by an unauthorized party: passwords could not be recovered (they are irreversibly hashed); the media server key could not be read (it is encrypted at rest); attendance records would be visible but meeting content would not be present — because it was never stored. The most sensitive items are protected even against direct database access.

What happens to a member's data if they leave the chapter?

A Chapter Admin can deactivate or remove a member account, immediately revoking their ability to log in or join meetings. Historical attendance records from past meetings remain as part of the unchangeable audit log — they record that a person attended past meetings, which may be required for accountability purposes. The former member's account information can be deleted at the chapter's discretion.

What if the server fails or is physically lost?

Meeting data is only as secure — and only as safe — as your server hardware. We strongly recommend regular encrypted backups of the database stored separately from the server itself. If the server is lost without a backup, meeting history would be lost. If the server is stolen, the protections described above (hashed passwords, encrypted secrets) still apply.

Do members need to trust us? Do they need to trust your chapter's IT administrator?

Members need to trust whoever operates the server — your chapter's technical administrator — in the same way they trust any organization that handles their personal information. We designed the system so that trust is placed in your chapter (an organization members already have a relationship with) rather than in a corporation they have never interacted with directly.

Could a future update compromise our privacy?

Updates are applied only when your chapter chooses to install them. Your chapter controls the version of the software it runs. We are not able to push changes to your server without your action. Your chapter can review any update before applying it, or choose not to update if a change is not acceptable.

What the code does — verifiable, not promised

The following are descriptions of how the current code is written. They are not legal warranties. Because the code is public, each one can be confirmed by anyone with programming knowledge:

Meeting content is not logged

The audit system records who joined or left, and when. It contains no mechanism to store audio, video, or discussion content. The relevant code is in AuditService and AuditMiddleware.

Passwords cannot be recovered

Member passwords are processed through BCrypt before storage. BCrypt is a one-way transformation — the original password cannot be derived from what is stored, even with full database access.

No outbound data transmission

The server code contains no calls to external analytics, telemetry, or reporting endpoints. Health metrics are served locally and never pushed to any outside service.

The key used to connect to the media server is stored encrypted using ASP.NET Data Protection. It cannot be read from the database as plaintext.
